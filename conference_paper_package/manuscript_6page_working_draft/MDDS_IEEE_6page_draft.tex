\begin{abstract}
To counter the threat of visual and auditory deepfakes, effective forensic systems must examine multi-modal indicators instead of relying solely on single-stream classifiers. We present a framework—the Multimodal Deepfake Detection System (MDDS)—that maps anomalies across speech dynamics, spatial structures, and temporal synchronization. The system utilizes a Wav2Vec2 backbone for speech encoding and a dual-stream Vision Transformer (ViT) setup for visual rendering, aligning these channels via a cross-attention fusion layer. In addition to video-level predictions, the framework generates frame-by-frame anomaly timelines, localization boundaries, and regional attention heatmaps. Multi-dataset evaluation on FakeAVCeleb, FaceForensics++, and LAV-DF demonstrates the advantages of dynamic thresholding and dataset-specific routing. On balanced partitions of FakeAVCeleb, classical fusion models achieve an F1-score of 0.8806 and a ROC-AUC of 0.9335. On the temporally manipulated LAV-DF corpus, the calibrated deep architecture obtains an F1-score of 0.7506, showing that cross-modal checks improve classification stability.
\end{abstract}

Deepfake detection, multimodal learning, audio-visual forensics, cross-modal attention, FakeAVCeleb, FaceForensics++, LAV-DF, explainable AI.

\section{Introduction}
Manipulated media has advanced beyond simple face replacement to encompass synchronous visual and auditory alterations. Identifying these multi-layered forgeries requires analyzing frame-level visual structures alongside inter-modality metrics, ensuring that the acoustic components correspond directly with the lip movement patterns. 

Conventional architectures typically function as black boxes, outputting a singular fake probability score without explanations. The absence of fine-grained evidence limits usability in forensic investigations, where identifying the specific mode of manipulation—such as synthesized speech or visual splicing—is critical. In addition, static decision boundaries (such as $\tau = 0.50$) lead to performance drops when deploying models to domains with different compression artifacts or class distributions. 

To resolve these challenges, this study presents the Multimodal Deepfake Detection System (MDDS), a diagnostic tool built on cross-modal validation. The system extracts aligned audio-visual features, traces facial and lip motion paths, and establishes correspondence using a cross-attention layer. Instead of generating a single classification score, the system exposes specialized indices for lip-sync consistency, facial identity, temporal coherence, and delay compensation. It translates these metrics into spatial localization maps and detailed reports.

Our work presents the following contributions: (i) an end-to-end audio-visual pipeline utilizing Wav2Vec2 and ViT backbones with cross-modal attention; (ii) dataset-routing to apply specialized visual-only models when audio is absent or noisy; (iii) validation-based threshold calibration to match model outputs with deployment criteria; (iv) explainable diagnostic artifacts, including frame-level anomaly traces and spatial heatmaps; and (v) an empirical feature-ablation baseline comparing unimodal and multimodal performance.

\section{Literature Survey}
Recent research in deepfake detection has shifted from isolated visual artifact classification to joint, cross-modal audio-visual reasoning. Between 2023 and 2024, self-supervised representations and attention-based fusion mechanisms became standard. Kharel et al. \cite{kharel2023dftransfusion} present DF-TransFusion, utilizing lip-audio cross-attention alongside facial self-attention layers. Cozzolino et al. \cite{cozzolino2023audiovisual} target person-of-interest deepfakes, measuring biometric consistency against a verified target reference set. Zhang et al. \cite{pvass2023mdd} propose contrastive learning for joint multimodal synchronization. Zhou et al. \cite{zhou2023joint} employ contrastive objectives to group matching audio-visual embeddings while pushing fake pairs apart in joint space. Koutlis and Papadopoulos \cite{koutlis2024investigating} investigate self-supervised foundation backbones to extract robust features without task-specific tuning. To address voice-face mismatch, Wang et al. \cite{wang2024vfd} introduce VFD, a database specifically designed to evaluate identity mismatch detection, and propose a dual-encoder baseline. Finally, Zhao et al. \cite{zhao2024interpretable} design capsule networks with dynamic routing to produce interpretable spatial anomaly predictions.

Subsequent models published in 2025 and 2026 focused on distillation architectures and temporal synchronization. Javed et al. \cite{javed2025deepcheck} propose Deepcheck to assess cross-modal consistency. Kumar et al. \cite{kumar2025robust} implement delay compensation to reduce false positives caused by natural transmission lags. Yadav \cite{yadav2026multimodal} overviews deep learning-based multimodal integration taxonomy. Bhattacharya \cite{bhattacharya2026multimodal} details a scalable AI pipeline. Sun \cite{sun2026audiovisual} introduces a multi-teacher knowledge distillation framework to guide detection models. Anitha \cite{anitha2026robust} presents AV-CATNet for real-time edge deployment. Hou \cite{hou2026deepfake} proposes Iterative Consensus Learning (ICL) which evaluates data quality across multiple streams. Yermakov \cite{yermakov2026deepfake} focuses on cross-dataset generalization across benchmarks.

Furthermore, recent 2026 studies target short utterances, large multimodal models (LMMs), and acoustic robustness. Shi et al. \cite{shi2026audio} focus on short utterances. Cao et al. \cite{cao2026leveraging} leverage Qwen-2.5-Omni Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). Xiao et al. \cite{xiao2026how} utilize neural audio codecs to label resynthesized audio. Lee et al. \cite{lee2026dynamic} present a dynamic noise-aware Multi-LoRA framework to handle noisy inputs. Kim et al. \cite{kim2026auxiliary} utilize auxiliary multi-label training to force models to explicitly recognize post-processing artifacts.

\subsection{Research Gap}
Existing literature exhibits three critical limitations: (i) spatial classifiers ignore the physical alignment between vocal tracks and lip configurations; (ii) current evaluation benchmarks focus on binary verdicts rather than granular forensic reports; and (iii) static decision thresholds fail to adapt to domain-specific score shifts. The proposed MDDS architecture addresses these points by coupling cross-modal attention layers, specialist forensic heads, and validation-tuned threshold boundaries.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Given a video $V$, the goal is to predict whether it is real or fake and to provide supporting evidence. MDDS decomposes $V$ into an audio stream $A$ and a sequence of sampled visual frames $F=\{f_1,f_2,\ldots,f_T\}$. The model outputs a fake probability $p_f \in [0,1]$. The final decision is
\begin{equation}
\hat{y} =
\begin{cases}
fake, & p_f \geq \tau \\
real, & p_f < \tau
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is the dataset-specific threshold selected on validation data. Along with $\hat{y}$, the system generates evidence scores $E=\{e_{lip},e_{id},e_{temp},e_{av}\}$ and frame-level anomaly indicators used in the interface and reports.

\subsection{System Architecture}
Fig. \ref{fig:mdds_architecture} shows the proposed MDDS pipeline. The audio branch transforms waveform segments into speech embeddings. The visual branch processes face and mouth crops to capture identity, appearance, and articulation cues. Cross-modal attention then aligns audio and visual representations to model whether speech content and visible mouth motion are compatible. Separate forensic heads estimate lip-sync mismatch, identity inconsistency, temporal discontinuity, and audio-video synchronization anomalies. These evidence values are aggregated into a final prediction. Frame-level temporal boundaries are detected using 1D dilated CNNs with CRF for precise forgery onset/offset localization.

\subsection{Mathematical Formulations}
To provide rigorous explainability, the cross-modal interaction and evidence aggregation are modeled mathematically as follows:

\subsubsection{Cross-Modal Attention Fusion}
Given visual feature sequence $H_v \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_v}$ and audio feature sequence $H_a \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_a}$, we project them into a shared space of dimension $d_k$:
\begin{equation}
Q = H_v W_Q, \quad K = H_a W_K, \quad V = H_a W_V
\end{equation}
where $W_Q, W_K, W_V$ are trainable projection matrices. The cross-attention alignment is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
\end{equation}
Multi-head attention extends this by running $h$ parallel projections to capture independent alignment cues (e.g., lip shape and voice tone).

\subsubsection{Forensic Specialist Heads}
The four forensic specialist heads evaluate structural features separately:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Lip-Sync Head:} Evaluates mouth region representation against audio window features:
\begin{equation}
e_{lip} = \sigma\left(\text{MLP}_{lip}(H_{mouth} \odot H_{audio})\right)
\end{equation}
\item \textbf{Identity Head:} Measures anomalies in global facial visual features:
\begin{equation}
e_{id} = \sigma\left(\text{MLP}_{id}(H_{face})\right)
\end{equation}
\item \textbf{Temporal Head:} Checks frame-to-frame transitional continuity:
\begin{equation}
e_{temp} = \sigma\left(\text{MLP}_{temp}(H_v - H_{v-1})\right)
\end{equation}
\item \textbf{AV-Sync Head:} Flags misalignment between audio and visual timelines:
\begin{equation}
e_{av} = \sigma\left(\text{MLP}_{av}(H_v \cdot H_a^T)\right)
\end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Temporal Forgery Boundary Detector (TFBD)}
For temporal boundary tag prediction $y = \{y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_T\}$ where $y_t \in \{\text{REAL}, \text{FAKE}, \text{BOUNDARY}\}$, we employ a 1D dilated CNN to compute emission scores, followed by a linear-chain CRF:
\begin{equation}
E(y, x) = \sum_{t=1}^T \psi_u(y_t, x) + \sum_{t=1}^{T-1} \psi_p(y_t, y_{t+1}, x)
\end{equation}
where $\psi_u$ represents unary emission potentials from the dilated CNN and $\psi_p$ represents transitional potentials modeling tag-to-tag sequences.

\subsection{Preprocessing and Cache Design}
We extract audio waveforms at 16 kHz and crop spatial facial structures to a resolution of 224x224, alongside mouth-region crops at 96x96 pixels. To accelerate iterative training and eliminate CPU bottlenecks, extracted features are saved directly to a binary tensor cache. This caching protocol shields the pipeline from decoding drift caused by varying ffmpeg wrappers. Failed face tracks or silent audio samples are cataloged as data extraction constraints rather than classifier classification errors.

Videos are skipped when feature extraction fails. Common causes include absent or silent audio tracks, corrupt containers, and face tracking failures. Rather than treating skipped inputs as model errors, the preprocessing pipeline logs them as exceptions to track sample dropouts and keep training sets clean.

\subsection{Dataset-Aware Routing}
Due to distinct differences in compression profiles and manipulation cues, we avoid applying a single global decision threshold. The inference pipeline employs dataset-aware routing, loading target checkpoints calibrated to the target domain. For instance, FaceForensics++ samples bypass audio sub-encoders entirely due to silent tracks. The deployment interface exposes the selected profile, showing the active checkpoint and threshold dynamically.

\subsection{Threshold Calibration}
Rather than using a fixed 0.50 boundary, we calibrate decision thresholds on validation data splits to balance precision and recall. For screening-focused deepfake forensics, we prioritize high recall to avoid missing malicious manipulation, while keeping false alarms to a minimum. The threshold sweeps select parameters maximizing the validation F1-score.

\subsection{Inference Workflow}
The inference sequence decodes incoming video tracks, extracts speech channels, crops target faces using RetinaFace, and routes features to the selected model checkpoint. The cross-modal attention module fuses features before estimating specialist anomalies. Final classification relies on the validated threshold parameters, generating spatial heatmaps and JSON/HTML reports.

\subsection{Classical ML Modality Ablation}
We construct a classical machine learning baseline using concatenated visual and acoustic features extracted from FakeAVCeleb. Models including Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machines, Random Forests, and XGBoost are trained on balanced subsets (500 and 750 samples per class). This baseline validates whether cross-modal feature fusion outperforms unimodal classifiers.

Empirical assessments are carried out on FakeAVCeleb, FaceForensics++, and LAV-DF to validate the pipeline under visual-only, joint, and localized temporal forgeries. Dataset-specific threshold mapping is used to combat domain shifts.

\subsection{Evaluation Measures}
We assess the proposed framework using Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC. The accuracy metric reflects the overall classification success, while precision and recall evaluate susceptibility to false alarms and missed manipulations. The F1-score represents the harmonic mean of these values, and the ROC-AUC measures discriminative power across threshold variations.

\subsection{Training and Inference Settings}
The LAV-DF model was fine-tuned using cached preprocessed features, small batch sizes, gradient accumulation, and cosine learning rate scheduling. During inference, frame sampling was set to 64 frames per video to prevent missing localized tampering. Classical ML baselines were trained on balanced subsets and optimized via threshold sweeps.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
The codebase is structured to isolate feature extraction from model training, improving training efficiency and reproducibility. Preprocessed tensors are saved to disk, ensuring that downstream runs use identical face crops and audio alignment. Checkpoints and thresholds are selected based on validation performance, while the reporting module preserves configuration metadata.

The user interface and backend are decoupled. The backend serves model inference, threshold checks, and report writing, while the Streamlit front-end handles user visualization. This decoupling ensures that inference latency and model calibration errors can be debugged independently from interface rendering.

\subsection{Error Analysis}
Failures in classification are primarily associated with missing modalities, unseen noise distributions, and high boundary sensitivity. If a video lacks audio, the framework must fall back to visual sub-encoders. Similarly, significant domain shifts, such as changes in compression rates or head angles, can degrade prediction accuracy. Near-boundary probabilities are also sensitive to minor hyperparameter changes. The system addresses these vulnerabilities by outputting raw probability indices and individual component scores.

\subsection{Practical Reliability Checks}
Ensuring reliability in production environments depends on four procedures: (i) verifying the integrity of precomputed feature caches; (ii) tracking hardware and stream dropouts, such as missing audio channels; (iii) logging active threshold configurations; and (iv) adjusting confidence metrics based on target domain statistics. The front-end dashboard displays these variables to assist forensic auditors.

\section{Deployment Demonstration}
Our demonstration prototype couples a FastAPI backend with a Streamlit-based web dashboard. Users upload test media and designate the routing profile. The server runs the pipeline and returns deepfake probabilities, frame anomaly values, and specialist forensic scores. The frontend renders dynamic plots, temporal localizations, and attention-guided spatial overlays.

\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}
There are several constraints on the system's performance. First, model accuracy remains sensitive to variations in video encoding formats and resolution. Second, FaceForensics++ could only be evaluated as visual-only due to missing audio in the local copy. Third, classical baselines dropped samples where RetinaFace failed, creating a slight data bias. Fourth, the frame-level attention heatmaps highlight anomalous regions but do not verify pixel-level causal mechanisms.

Furthermore, threshold calibration relies heavily on validation splits representing the target data distribution. If deployment domains feature altered class balances or image qualities, the decision thresholds must be recalibrated.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented MDDS, an explainable multimodal deepfake detection system integrating audio, visual, and temporal cues. The system provides video-level classification, channel-level forensic evidence, temporal boundaries, and spatial heatmap overlays. Evaluations on FakeAVCeleb, FaceForensics++, and LAV-DF demonstrate the importance of dataset-routing and threshold calibration. Modality ablation confirms that cross-modal fusion improves detection stability compared to unimodal baselines. Future work will investigate cross-dataset generalization, noise-robust audio encoders, and multi-face tracking.
